# Chapter 3: On Knowledge

### The Shared Knowledge Foundation

One of the most crucial elements in human deliberation is our shared knowledge base — the foundation that helps us evaluate options when pursuing our goals. However, each person's knowledge base differs, creating an intricate web of diverse understandings. When we lack agreement on how the world works, conflicts naturally arise. This becomes particularly evident in everyday decision-making and is amplified when people from different cultural backgrounds and worldviews try to work together.

### The Phenomenological Limitation

Our starting point is simple yet profound: we experience the world through our senses and interpret it through our minds. Following Kant's philosophy, we never directly access reality (the "thing-in-itself") but only experience phenomena — our perception of reality. When eating ice cream, for instance, we experience coldness and flavor. But we cannot definitively prove whether these sensations come from actual ice cream, a computer simulation, or a dream. All our experiences are filtered through our minds and senses.

This leads to an interesting dilemma: each person lives in their own mental world, interpreting reality uniquely. This perspective finds strong support in postmodern thought. Jean-François Lyotard, in *The Postmodern Condition* (1979), argues against grand narratives and universal truth claims, suggesting instead that knowledge is inherently localized and contextual. Similarly, Jacques Derrida's concept of *différance* emphasizes how meaning is constantly deferred and differs between interpretations, making absolute truth claims problematic. Michel Foucault's analysis of knowledge-power relationships further demonstrates how what counts as "truth" is deeply embedded in social and historical contexts.

### Beyond Relativistic Paralysis

Some philosophers, particularly postmodernists, stop here, concluding that everyone has their own truth and there is no way to bridge these different realities. Jean Baudrillard takes this to its logical extreme in *Simulacra and Simulation* (1981), arguing that in our postmodern world, we have lost all connection to the "real," living instead in a world of simulations. While not strictly postmodern, Richard Rorty similarly argues in *Philosophy and the Mirror of Nature* (1979) that we should abandon the search for objective truth and instead focus on practical consequences. However, this view makes meaningful dialogue and cooperation nearly impossible.

To move beyond this impasse, we can employ Occam's Razor — the principle of choosing the simplest explanation that fits our observations. Not because the simplest answer is necessarily true, but because it is practical and testable. Consider the classic example of swans: if we observe only white swans for years, we might conclude that all swans are white. When we encounter a black swan, we face a choice. We could construct elaborate theories to preserve our original belief, or adopt a simpler explanation: swans come in black and white.

### The Collaborative Testing Framework

This approach, combined with Karl Popper's principle of falsification, gives us a practical method for building and testing theories about the world. We form hypotheses that can be proven wrong, then test them against our observations. When they fail, we revise rather than defend them with increasingly complex justifications.

This process works not just individually but collectively. Following Edmund Husserl's insight in *Cartesian Meditations*, we can reasonably assume that other people possess similar cognitive abilities and can engage in the same process of observation and reasoning. Occam's Razor does not help us see reality more clearly, since we cannot directly access objective truth. But it gives us a shared framework for working together, testing ideas, and building knowledge collaboratively.

Consider a practical example: imagine two people trying to explain why sheep are getting sick. One suggests demons are responsible; another blames bacteria. Rather than arguing abstractly, we can test both theories. We could conduct a controlled experiment comparing one group receiving treatment for bacterial infection with an untreated control group, following Koch's postulates. Whichever explanation better predicts and explains our observations becomes more useful, regardless of its philosophical underpinnings.

### The Humility of Knowledge

This method does not automatically favor scientific theories, though they often perform well under such testing. Sometimes, non-scientific explanations prove valuable in specific contexts — for instance, studies show that religious beliefs can help people cope better with trauma. This suggests that different types of knowledge serve various purposes.

The beauty of this approach is that it provides common ground for people with different worldviews to work together and test their ideas. However, we must remain sensitive to how challenging it can be when fundamental beliefs are questioned. Whether religious or secular, people can feel threatened when their core beliefs are challenged by evidence that suggests alternative approaches might work better.

To overcome such difficulties, intellectual modesty may be the key. We must remember that we cannot determine with certainty whether the world was created according to scientific theories beginning with the Big Bang, or whether a supreme being created the world 5,785 years ago in six days. A creationist might argue that the supreme being "created the world with its past" — generating all the fossil evidence and apparent evolutionary history at once. We also cannot know if we are living in a Matrix, because whoever created such a reality also created our logic and memory. Yet scientific theories offer the most straightforward solutions to the various evidence before us. We should prefer verified knowledge over unverified — not because it is inherently more true, but because it can be tested and verified by all. We should be humble in our aspirations for truth and leave room for other opinions. This modesty may be the key to our ability to work together despite differences in worldview.

### Inter-Perspective Deliberation

Inter-perspective observation allows us to examine each claim from different perspectives, refute it, and find new theories that better withstand inter-perspective refutation tests. This way, we can arrive at more agreed-upon claims. We can also develop more corroborated theories for planning joint action by checking them through observations and experiments.

Additionally, inter-perspective deliberation can expose the needs of all participants and thus enable the search for solutions that will improve everyone's situation when action is taken based on mutual agreement.

This humble approach should allow laypeople to express their inner feelings and beliefs and discuss the theories brought to the table by experts. Both ideas should be tested and corroborated, acknowledging that we have no ownership of the truth — only mutual exploration of a shared field. We should put our theories to the test and humbly try to arrive at the best corroborated theories needed for taking joint action.

Multi-participant, inter-perspective deliberation is a complex process that is challenging for most people. Articulating a simple and sharp position is much easier than maintaining a nuanced and complex view.

### The SON-MON Framework

Finding a shared language and constructing discussions that can converge and be implemented through technology requires understanding how we perceive the world and how this can be represented in computational systems.

Let us begin with one of the most basic learning processes in the human brain: Long-Term Potentiation. In this process, a neuron connected to many other neurons simultaneously receives stimulation from two different neurons. If this pattern repeats from the same two neurons over various intervals, the number of synapses connecting them increases, creating a triangular connection between the two stimulating neurons and the base neuron. This is how, neurology suggests, fundamental learning occurs.

From a philosophical perspective, there are several correlations. Like the central nervous system, we never actually see reality directly. We are trapped in a phenomenological cage (Reichenbach, Kant), with no knowledge of what occurs outside it. However, when we receive an auditory phenomenon processed by the brain as "barking" while simultaneously receiving a visual phenomenon interpreted as a dog opening and closing its mouth, we create a connection between barking and dog. Through abduction, we establish this link. Using this connection, we can now assume through induction that "all dogs bark." Our brain identifies this relationship through neuronal connection, which forms when there are repeated co-occurrences of similar events.

The dog is not connected only to barking. Perhaps every time we see a dog, we also see that it has a tail, and through abduction we can form the rule "all dogs have tails." In essence, our reality consists of objects that are connected in our minds to various interpretations of phenomena. We hold clusters of such inductions — what can be called a **Mental Object (MO)** — describing the perceived world for us.

These clusters are also interconnected. For example, we can hold a mental object of an apple, connected to specific smell, taste, shape, and colors. We can hold mental objects of trees, describing appearance, permanence in place, shade, etc. We can connect between them and create "apple tree" — meaning that some trees produce apples. We might also know that a group of apple trees is called an "apple orchard," and through abduction we can know that humans cultivate apple orchards. With many such connections of mental objects created through abduction, forming clusters of interconnected object-inductions, we arrive at what I call a **Mental Object Network (MON)**. Each of us has their own personal MON.

Through following the connections between these networked mental objects, we can perform deduction. "If I see an orchard of apple trees, I can deduce that someone planted this orchard."

However, both inductions and deductions can be refuted. For example, if as a child I had a dog that was my true friend, I might build the assumption that "all dogs are friendly." But if one day another dog attacks me, I learn that the induction "all dogs are friendly" has been refuted. I can deal with this challenge in various ways. I can try to save the existing theory by adding an "auxiliary theory" — a theory whose sole purpose is to protect my basic assumption. Or I can build a theory that fits more of the results I have experienced, such as "there are friendly dogs and unfriendly dogs." As Popper and Mach suggested, following Occam's principle of parsimony, a theory is only the most economical description of the totality of observations.

In other words, when our theories are refuted, we must reorganize our mental objects and rebuild them so they fit as economically as possible with the totality of our observations.

But naturally, most people resist this. Many prefer to save their theories and find auxiliary explanations to preserve their basic assumptions. Learning and reorganizing MONs is a demanding task — our theories are probably connected to our well-being and self-esteem. Many of us prefer to see our own ideas as correct and others' as misjudgments.

Refuting old theories and constructing new ones is essential for progress. According to the principle of parsimony, if nature is governed by laws, then replacing outdated theories with ones that better match our observations is crucial for survival. The brain naturally follows this approach: it is built to update, reorganize, and adapt continuously.

However, once writing was invented, this process changed. Instead of relying only on flexible, local theories shaped by immediate experience, we began to create far more complex and abstract theories — made possible by the permanence of writing. These theories became harder to replace. Over time, they formed the foundations of cultures and societies. Challenging or discarding them was no longer just an intellectual act, but a social threat.

For example, whether a society believes in one god, many gods, or none at all is not simply a matter of personal thought — it can determine whether that society holds together or dissolves. Through writing, our private, flexible theories of the mind were transformed into shared, rigid frameworks — what might be called **Social Object Networks (SONs)**. Oral cultures tended to keep these frameworks more flexible, but once writing took hold, SONs hardened. Changing them became increasingly difficult. Since these shared theories underpin social organization itself, altering them is often experienced as threatening, destabilizing, and at times even dangerous.

Even at the community level, shared words and fields of knowledge carry conflicting interests, and can threaten the personal standing of participants. Different participants, each with their own interests, prefer to frame reality in ways that steer others toward their desired goals. As a result, discussions often quickly turn into power struggles, and the search for truth — within its epistemological limits — can vanish almost instantly.

If we want to sustain healthy deliberation — one that includes everyone's needs and strives for the best possible understanding of reality so that we can make good decisions — we must first recognize the challenges that stand before us.

There are several ways to reduce the combativeness over "narrative" and encourage open, honest, and critical discussion. In science, for example, we learned early to distinguish between *ad hominem* and *ad rem*. When discussing evidence that everyone can examine, the discussion becomes somewhat less emotional. The use of writing, rather than oral conversation, allows colleagues to examine claims and refute or corroborate them as needed.

From a social perspective, tensions can be lowered through epistemological humility, which reduces the threat to social structures. Another way to reduce tension is to avoid, as much as possible, decisions that are binding on everyone. Degrees of freedom in choosing solutions allow participants to select the approach that seems most appropriate to them.

### Technology

Technology can help us, if we adapt it to our needs. As we have seen, putting ideas into writing helps us articulate our thoughts more clearly, allowing us to refine and reconsider before we commit to their final form. Once written, others can read our understanding, comment on it, and suggest refutations or corroborations. This is how human knowledge has advanced over the last 2,600 years, and especially in the last 400 years since the scientific revolution. Yet today, we can take these knowledge systems and reasoning processes even further. Building software that supports better reasoning is now achievable.

Let me start with some basic building blocks. Objects are foundational elements of software architecture. In many programming languages, there are structures that store data using something very similar to the mental objects described above: objects store properties (keys) and the values of those properties, and they can reference other objects, creating networks of relationships. This makes them an ideal starting point for our technological framework.

Yet one crucial difference exists between basic computer objects and biological ones: in computers, the key-value relationship is binary (true or false), while in biological mental objects, the strength of connections between objects varies along a continuum. This variable strength indicates how reliable or familiar a relationship is within our Mental Object Network (MON).

Strength is a crucial property because it reflects how much we can rely on a given connection. The more we test a link — for instance, every time we drink coffee it makes us alert, day after day — the stronger and more reliable that link becomes. But if we try a new remedy that someone recommended and we have never tested, the link will be weak and we will rely on it less.

Graph databases, which can represent the strength of a link, offer an available foundation for creating digital SONs. Utilizing such architecture can help us represent human knowledge needed for deliberation in a more reliable way. Yet we still need to devise ways to bridge between observations and the optimization of the structure of digital SONs.

In our application FreeDi, we used objects to represent ideas (we called them "statements"), and we used the measure of consensus to represent the strength of the links between ideas. The more consensus an idea had, the stronger the link. This method also helped us create a network of linked objects, connected by the strength of a link. For instance, we start with a question: "What is the best way to solve an issue?" Then everyone suggests a solution (option). People evaluate how strongly they support or oppose each option, and the more consensus an option has, the stronger the link. We calculate consensus by the average support (on a scale of -1 to 1) times the square root of the number of people who evaluated that option.

When a user views the question from the top level, they see only the options with the most consensus — the accepted solution or solutions. They can then go to that solution and ask further questions, again evaluated for consensus, with the highest-consensus responses rising to the top. In this infinite process, a SON of questions is translated into linked objects.

Yet such architecture is not enough to support the full process of deliberation. The current architecture assumes that the whole team shares a single SON. In reality, we need to accommodate different MONs and SONs rather than enforcing a single unified framework. We need to enable negotiation between diverse SONs and MONs to find the optimal shared framework — or frameworks — that enable effective collaboration. For instance, marketing and development teams hold different SONs, yet they must agree on a product's strategic direction. We need an architecture that supports multiple SONs and MONs while facilitating negotiation to create hybrid SONs that enable collaboration.

Additionally, while FreeDi bases the strength between objects on consensus alone, we need more sophisticated measures. To address critical factors like reliability, probability of success, ROI, and values, we must incorporate multidimensional metrics into our strength calculations.

Furthermore, we need the ability to reconstruct entire theories. Consider the classic example: we hold a theory that all swans are white, represented as an object containing an inductive link between "swan" and "white." When we encounter black swans, we face a choice. We might revise our theory to state "swans are either black or white," or we might construct an entirely new theory proposing distinct species of swan-like birds. In the latter case, we must create new theoretical objects and relationships. We need an architecture that allows us to replace entire theoretical frameworks seamlessly.

A more complex example is the paradigm shift from Newtonian to modern physics. Such transitions require reconstructing our entire SON to accommodate fundamentally different theoretical foundations. Developing the capability to translate between human theories and their digital representations — and to transform one theoretical framework into another — is a crucial challenge.

If we achieve this, we will possess powerful tools for developing stronger theories through abductive reasoning, enabling the system to generate and test novel hypotheses. Just as writing combined with scientific methodology enhanced our ability to understand the physical world, building reasoning mechanisms based on dynamic SONs could represent a breakthrough in our capacity to address complex social problems.

This is only the beginning. Other elements should be added to create better digital SONs: evaluation of probability, reliability, ROI, and group members' values. The network should be agile, enabling it to shift as group knowledge changes and adapt over time.

To be able to do so, we need to further investigate group reasoning and decision-making. That is the subject of our next chapter.
