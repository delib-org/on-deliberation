# Chapter 1: On the Crisis of Democratic Coordination

*Why traditional hierarchical decision-making fails to serve diverse communities, and the exponential complexity problem of group coordination*

### The Historical Context of Coordination

Humans have worked in groups for hundreds of thousands of years. Group work improves survival and increases the ability to thrive. However, group collaboration requires significant coordination and expertise, often demanding substantial resources to implement effectively.

Coordinating is highly complex, requiring a sophisticated deliberation culture and extensive knowledge of coordination methods. In tribal cultures, there is evidence of refined deliberation processes that enable members to make and thoroughly accept collaborative decisions. However, as human organizations grew, these deliberation processes became increasingly complex and time-consuming, necessitating the development of alternative methods.

Consequently, a practice emerged that concentrated group decision-making in the hands of hierarchical subgroups. We see this organizational pattern throughout human society: from patriarchal or matriarchal families to community leadership councils, from hierarchically managed businesses to governments with prime ministers, cabinets, and legislative bodies — all representing a small fraction of the total population they serve.

### The Limitations of Hierarchical Decision-Making

Although this hierarchical decision-making system has served humanity for thousands of years, enabling the establishment of thriving businesses and national entities comprising millions or even billions of individuals, this centralized system still has significant room for improvement.

The concentration of decision-making power in the hands of a tiny minority almost inevitably means that the interests of this small group will take precedence over those of the vast majority. In societies lacking proper checks and balances on decision-makers, the disparities between those close to power and the general public can become staggering.

Even in places with established checks and balances and democratically elected officials, it remains remarkably difficult for the public to effectively monitor their chosen representatives. Furthermore, decision-makers face an overwhelming amount of information, making it challenging for a small group to process and comprehend all the data necessary for making well-informed decisions that would optimally serve the public interest.

Modern organizations face an increasingly complex challenge: while hierarchical decision-making has enabled unprecedented coordination among billions of people, it often fails to serve the diverse interests of entire communities adequately.

### The Failure of Current Deliberative Culture

To preserve the interests of the general public and ensure decisions benefit society optimally, we should find ways to consider the needs and interests of all citizens and discover solutions that serve every member of the group.

One of humanity's most valuable tools for finding its path forward is deliberation. Deliberation enables group members to discuss issues relevant to the decisions at hand and identify solutions that benefit the group as a whole. However, this art faces many challenges today.

A debate culture has largely taken its place, where one side must be perfectly correct and the other wrong. Yet reality is far more complex. Exploring reality with only black and white loses the many nuances needed to understand complex issues. When one side wins and the other loses in such debates, the portion of the group that inevitably loses may feel estranged or that their interests have been harmed, which can create lasting rifts.

### The Problem with Current Coordination Methods

Alternative approaches to coordination exist in current systems. For instance, many debates devolve into each side attempting to prove its absolute rightness and the other's absolute wrongness. We also see propaganda and information manipulation, where parties use various media tools to demonstrate how "reality" aligns with their worldview and argue that acting according to their understanding will yield better results. Some resort to intimidation and coercion, forcing public compliance with their demands.

While these methods exist and can achieve some level of coordination, they are far from optimal for genuine group coordination.

The fundamental problem with debate, propaganda, and coercion is their assumption that reality is simple. They presume that if people could only grasp their particular group's insight, they would understand the "truth" and willingly follow that group's approach. However, decades of research and millennia of philosophical discourse have revealed that reality is far more complex.

Our attempts to understand how best to advance society demonstrate this complexity. Anyone who has started a business or operated as an independent service provider knows that success requires nuanced understanding and constant adaptation. One must comprehend customer needs, engage effectively with employees and managers, and respond to constantly emerging market phenomena. As Kant observed, "reality itself" — the "thing in itself" — is more complex than humans can grasp, and may be entirely unattainable if we accept his perspective.

The recognition that reality defies simple explanations brings us face-to-face with a sobering truth: even when people genuinely want to collaborate and find optimal solutions together, the deliberative process itself presents formidable obstacles. To design systems that can transcend these limitations, we must first understand what makes group decision-making so exponentially difficult — and why traditional approaches inevitably break down as participation scales.

### The Exponential Complexity Problem

The complexity of group decision-making grows exponentially with each additional member that requires coordination.

#### Communication Bottlenecks

When two people need to make a decision through a phone conversation, they require one communication line that they must share. If they have one hour available, each can speak for an average of half an hour. In practice, one might speak 90% of the time and the other 10%, but they cannot speak simultaneously — they must divide the available time over a single communication channel.

When a third person joins the decision-makers, personal phone conversations now require three communication lines instead of one. All participants now have only one-sixth of the original time for interpersonal coordination. As group size increases, the number of required coordination lines grows according to the following formula:

> **Communication lines = n(n-1)/2**

This represents exponential growth. As more people join the decision-making circle, the time available for each person to communicate their understanding decreases exponentially.

#### Alternative Communication Formats and Their Limitations

It is possible to increase conversation time through different communication formats. One alternative involves a medium where everyone can hear each other simultaneously — for example, conducting the discussion in a single hall where all participants hear each speaker. In this case, speaking time per participant decreases linearly rather than exponentially as the group grows.

However, this format significantly impairs communication quality. When disagreements arise between two participants, they lack dedicated time to coordinate their understanding privately. If they attempt to resolve their disagreement publicly, they consume time that prevents the entire group from continuing its coordination. This dynamic is familiar to anyone who has sat through team meetings where two people argue while others wait restlessly for the discussion to return to the broader agenda.

This limitation can be partially addressed through parallel conversations in smaller groups — an approach exemplified by Open Space Technology gatherings. Participants move freely between conversation groups based on their interests, and when working on large shared projects, personal conversations can occur without consuming collective time, achieving better overall synchronization.

Another improvement involves transitioning from synchronous communication — where one person speaks while others listen — to asynchronous formats. Written discussion forums, for example, allow each person to contribute at their own pace and respond thoughtfully in their own time. This significantly increases the time available for each person to express themselves, process information, and reflect.

However, written communication introduces its own complications. Much non-verbal information is lost, making it difficult to convey and interpret emotions, detect nuances, and understand social cues. Moreover, written communication feels less natural than conversation for most people, causing many to struggle with effective expression or to avoid participating altogether. The permanent nature of written records — where mistakes remain visible indefinitely — further inhibits many people from expressing themselves freely.

Regardless of the communication format chosen, overall group synchronization becomes increasingly difficult as participation grows.

#### Beyond Communication: The Deeper Complexity Challenge

The increase in complexity stems not only from the need to share communication time, but from a more fundamental challenge: each participant enters the discussion with their own worldview, language, needs, goals, conversational culture, and learning capabilities. These differences make coordination exponentially more complex and time-consuming.

The challenge of creating shared understanding multiplies with each new participant. Group members must develop common language, aligned worldviews, and shared conversational values. They must identify mutual interests and common goals while establishing learning methods that enable effective communication. Each of these coordination requirements grows significantly with every addition to the decision-making circle.

Consequently, the time available for individuals to actively participate in shaping shared understanding, building solutions, and selecting among alternatives appears to decrease exponentially as new people join the decision-making process.

#### Societal Adaptations and Their Limitations

Human societies have developed several mechanisms to reduce decision-making complexity. Conversational cultures that develop within communities enable members to coordinate how, when, and in what manner to communicate so that different parties remain prepared to listen constructively. Each community develops its own vocabulary, and over time, unique languages emerge that facilitate internal communication while creating barriers to external coordination.

This linguistic evolution probably represents one of the foundations for the development of distinct languages. While different conversational cultures and languages make it easier for group members to make joint decisions internally, they simultaneously complicate communication and decision-making between communities and peoples.

This dynamic affects multicultural societies like the United States, Europe, and Israel, which absorb immigrants from diverse backgrounds. Even when common languages emerge, cultural communication difficulties persist because conversational styles and discourse norms vary significantly across cultures.

Creating shared discourse that crosses cultural and organizational boundaries therefore requires deliberately developing common discussion cultures. These must be designed thoughtfully to encourage widespread adoption while enabling higher-quality decision-making — a concept we will explore in subsequent chapters.

#### The Persistent Challenge of Scale

Despite all the mechanisms human societies have developed to improve collective decision-making, complexity continues to grow exponentially with the number of participants involved in the process. This fundamental limitation explains why effective group decision-making becomes increasingly difficult beyond certain thresholds and why solutions that work smoothly for small groups often break down in larger teams.

This natural constraint has profoundly shaped how human societies organize themselves. From corporate executive boards to government cabinets, from military command structures to university administrations, we consistently observe critical decisions concentrated in the hands of relatively small groups. This pattern is not arbitrary — it represents a practical response to cognitive and coordination limitations.

While teams of five to nine people can engage in nuanced discussion and reach consensus through direct interaction, groups of 50 or 100 attempting to make complex decisions together often result in gridlock or superficial agreements that fail during implementation. Consider how even in our most democratic institutions — legislatures, shareholder meetings, community organizations — the detailed work of decision-making typically falls to smaller committees or executive teams who then present refined proposals to larger groups.

This concentration of decision-making authority, while pragmatically necessary, creates the fundamental challenge that this framework seeks to address: how to maintain genuine democratic participation while managing the exponential complexity of large-group coordination.

### Technological Solutions for the Coordination Challenge

As we have seen in the preceding sections, the medium through which we communicate can significantly impact the quality and speed of coordination. Phone conversations and face-to-face meetings are high-quality tools for managing interpersonal discussions, but they are poorly suited for multi-participant coordination. Conversely, asynchronous forums help increase the number of participants but reduce discussion quality and equitable participation.

Today, with advances in technology, notable improvements in artificial intelligence, and the growing ease of developing technological products, we as a society can develop solutions that enable easier coordination. For example, it is possible to develop an asynchronous medium that facilitates participation by allowing people to write their thoughts while artificial intelligence reformulates their messages to make them clearer and more digestible for readers. AI can also add contextual cues to better clarify social intentions.

Similarly, in conventional forums, lengthy discussions on topics and subtopics can become exhausting. Artificial intelligence can read discussion threads and summarize them for those who simply want to understand what was discussed. Through technology like what we developed at FreeDi, it is also possible to see what was agreed upon and what was not in a given thread — making discussions more readable despite the proliferation of text.

To develop such tools and efficient discussion cultures and processes that will enable us to significantly expand the circle of decision-makers, we must develop a deep understanding of deliberation, communication mediums, and the ways in which people communicate. This will be the focus of the next chapters.

### The Difficulty of Studying and Developing Deliberation

To develop our deliberation capabilities, we need to understand deliberation itself. However, the field of deliberation is challenging to research. It exists in the soft areas of social sciences where, at this stage, clear "laws of nature" have not yet been established, and it is difficult to describe the forces and laws operating in human interaction.

It is therefore difficult to identify which variables influence which, and what "laws" govern those interactions. While we have made progress and have quite a few insights about various influences, we still lack a precise mechanism to describe the different interactions in deliberation and their effects.

Within this book, I present a new framework for researching and understanding deliberation through the synthesis of research from cognitive science, epistemology (the philosophy of knowledge), sociology, and related fields.

After presenting these underlying mechanisms, I will demonstrate how we can build discussion methodologies and deliberative technologies that harness these mechanisms to create higher-quality, more democratic deliberation.

This approach offers a path beyond the traditional limitations that have constrained democratic participation throughout history. By understanding how individual minds process information and make decisions, and the mechanisms and interests of group decision-making, we can design systems that amplify rather than diminish collective intelligence — allowing us to reach broader and more effective deliberation.
