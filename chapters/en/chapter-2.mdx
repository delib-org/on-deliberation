# Chapter 2: On the Nature of Human Deliberation

*The cognitive science foundations — how individuals make decisions, the role of knowledge bases, needs, preferences, and learning*

### The Foundation of Decision-Making

To understand the complex dynamics of group decision-making, we must first examine how individuals make choices. The individual decision-making process serves as the fundamental building block, shaping how people evaluate options, weigh consequences, and ultimately arrive at conclusions. Just as atoms form molecules, individual cognitive processes and biases combine and interact when people make decisions collectively. This sequential approach — starting with the individual before expanding to group dynamics — allows us to trace how personal judgment patterns both contribute to and transform within group settings.

### The Architecture of Individual Choice

#### Needs: The Genesis of Action

Decision-making begins with the recognition of an unmet need. When people identify a gap between their current state and desired state, they feel compelled to take action. This leads to the crucial step of deciding how to proceed — evaluating different approaches and choosing the most effective path to satisfy the need. The decision-making process transforms the initial awareness of a need into a concrete plan for fulfillment.

The greater the need, the stronger the drive to take action to fulfill it.

#### Resources: The Currency of Choice

To fulfill their needs, decision-makers must invest certain resources. For example, if a person needs to travel to work, they will need to invest resources to choose a transportation method, whether driving (requiring fuel, vehicle maintenance, and attention) or taking public transportation (requiring fare payment, walking to stations, and time management). Generally, unless we truly enjoy the process itself, we seek to invest the minimum resources required to meet our needs. In order to optimally choose how to invest resources to fulfill needs, we require knowledge about the ways in which things work.

#### Knowledge and Outcomes: The Map of Possibility

Understanding how to act is based on knowledge stored in the brain about optional actions to meet a given need. The person will examine different possibilities and check the outcomes of each. Each action is connected in their mind to consequences. One of those consequences is meeting their need, but there are additional consequences — for example, what resources will be required to perform the task, and whether consuming these resources is worth the benefit. There are also accompanying consequences whose benefits or drawbacks can harm or benefit the person's general situation.

For example, if a person feels hungry, they can choose a cake waiting in the refrigerator or a slice of low-calorie bread. Eating the cake — which is intended for their daughter's birthday — means they will need to buy a new one for 250 shekels and spend time and money on the trip. The positive consequences are that the food will be delicious and comforting, but the negative consequences include weight gain and its social and health implications. On the other hand, eating bread will help maintain weight. There is no need to buy a replacement, and the cost is minimal. But the pleasure of enjoying indulgent food disappears.

When people need to decide, they evaluate possibilities and consequences and determine which decision will benefit them most overall.

The cognitive state of the decision-maker influences prioritization between possibilities. When cognitive resources are depleted, we tend to make impulsive decisions that benefit us in the short term but may harm us in the long term — at such times, we are more likely to prefer cake over fruit. When our regulatory resources are in good condition and cognitive energy is high, we tend toward possibilities that benefit us in the long term, even at the cost of short-term gains.[^1]

#### Probability and Corroboration: The Lens of Uncertainty

When making decisions, we must carefully evaluate the probability of each potential outcome. This probability assessment is influenced by two key components: the level of corroboration of our knowledge about the subject, and the inherent likelihood of the outcome itself.

The first component — our experiential knowledge — becomes more reliable as we gain expertise in a particular domain. For instance, a senior software developer can often estimate project timelines and potential technical challenges more accurately than a junior programmer. Their years of experience lead to more reliable probability assessments — for example, whether a new feature might introduce bugs or how long a complex integration will take. As our understanding grows, our ability to estimate probabilities becomes more refined and stable.

The second component involves the underlying probability distribution of the outcome itself, which exists independently of our knowledge. Consider the traffic flow at a major city intersection during rush hour. While GPS navigation might suggest a 15-minute drive, the actual travel time follows a natural probability distribution. A local delivery driver might observe, "Between 8–9 AM, there's a greater chance it takes 20–25 minutes and a minor chance of it taking over 30 minutes if there's an incident." These probabilities emerge from inherent factors like traffic light timing, volume of vehicles, pedestrian crossings, and driver behaviors — all interacting in complex but somewhat predictable patterns. Estimating these natural probability distributions is part of our evaluation of potential outcomes when choosing how to act.

#### Evaluation and Preferences: The Weight of Values

Individual preferences shape how we evaluate and prioritize different outcomes, reflecting our unique constellation of needs and desires. These preferences emerge from a complex interplay of factors: our genetic predispositions, learned behaviors from social environments, subjective interpretations of what fulfills us, and our current state of need satisfaction. For instance, someone may prefer high-intensity activities due to a genetic predisposition toward sensation-seeking, reinforced by social circles that value adventurous pursuits and amplified by a perceived lack of stimulation in daily life. Conversely, another person might gravitate toward contemplative activities due to an innate inclination toward introspection, cultural influences that prize scholarly pursuits, and a self-perceived need for mental enrichment.

These preferences are not static — they fluctuate based on our current state of fulfillment and environmental conditions. Just as physical hunger intensifies our preference for food, emotional or intellectual deprivation can strengthen our drive toward activities that address those specific deficits. This dynamic nature of preferences explains why our choices often shift as our circumstances and satisfaction levels change.

When making decisions, our needs and preferences form an interconnected system where satisfaction in one area affects desires in others. Rather than operating from fixed priorities, we constantly recalibrate based on our current state of fulfillment. This dynamic interplay of competing and complementary needs shapes choices that maximize overall well-being through an unconscious cost-benefit analysis.

For example, a desire for career advancement might compete with a preference for work-life balance. The decision to work late on a project versus spending time with family depends not just on isolated preferences for career success or family time, but on how these preferences interact with our current level of fulfillment in each area. The action we choose is the one that best harmonizes with this dynamic interplay of preferences and needs at that specific moment.

#### Learning and Improvement: The Evolution of Choice

Learning often stems from recognizing gaps between our needs and the available solutions.

Cognitive research has shown that there is a significant gap between how things actually behave and how we perceive them. We have many documented perceptual errors. This collection of errors can lead us to significant mistakes in understanding how to solve our challenges and in executing solutions. The way to learn and correct these biases is, as Karl Popper suggested, to hypothesize, try, fail, and learn. Repeated experiences and the mistakes we discover can reveal that we erred in our initial assumptions, and we must correct them so they better fit the actual behavior of the world around us. Through this ongoing process of trial and error, we build a more accurate picture of how things work.

Marshall McLuhan argued that writing is one of our tools for transforming abstract thinking into linear thinking. Building on this insight, I propose as a hypothesis that writing may be an important tool in our ability to make theories more well-founded, and therefore more capable of predicting — within the philosophical limitations of knowledge — what might happen. If we use an effective system of hypotheses, experiments, and critiques, we can develop an even more accurate understanding of the laws operating in nature. This is essentially how the scientific method works.

Building further on this insight, I raise the hypothesis that Kahneman and Tversky may not have fully distinguished between innate cognitive processes and capabilities acquired through external tools. System 1, with all its heuristics and biases, appears to be the natural way the brain operates. In contrast, what they called "System 2" may be partly a product of external tools — especially writing and methodological calculation techniques.

My hypothesis is that the ability to solve complex problems, such as those Kahneman and Tversky presented to their research participants, relies largely on capabilities that people developed after learning to calculate and analyze in writing. When experiment participants performed the required analytical processes, they may have been drawing on knowledge and methodologies acquired when learning to think methodologically — a process that indeed required greater engagement of working memory and caused pupil dilation, but whose origin lies in technology developed thousands of years ago rather than in the brain's innate mechanisms.

If this hypothesis is correct, it offers an important insight: the use of technology — from writing to modern digital tools — may enable us to advance our capabilities to process information more reliably. This topic will require further research, and we will elaborate on the opportunities it presents in the chapters that follow.

### Technological Foundation to Improve This Process

Based on our understanding of these cognitive components in decision-making processes, we can build technology that assists decision-making in a more natural way. For now, we will discuss a decision-support system at the individual level; later, when we address group decision-making, we will adapt it for collective decisions.

Currently, we tend to choose among a limited number of options — usually those we know well or that have been presented to us, for example, between Republicans and Democrats, or between pizza and an apple. However, the solution space for any problem is philosophically infinite. Therefore, a decision-making system must accommodate unlimited possibilities. It should begin with the option space familiar to the decision-maker and gradually expand alternatives when they fail to produce optimal solutions.

For each option, we must evaluate its level of corroboration, its probability of success, the required investment, whether the decision-maker possesses these resources or can mobilize them, the potential benefit, and the return on investment (ROI).

If we can build such a system, it could help identify diverse solutions within the possibility space that would benefit the user. Technological systems have the advantage of being able to remember more information, maintain more solution options, and filter through vast arrays of different possibilities, providing decision-makers with an excellent tool to assist in the decision-making process.

The system must, of course, know the user and understand their value system, which guides their prioritization. If a user currently needs a quick solution and has no financial constraints, the system might identify fast but expensive solutions. Conversely, for a user with limited financial resources who still needs a quick solution, the system would identify cheap and fast alternatives. The system could weigh different preferences and offer personalized solutions.

Such systems could form the foundation for developing multi-participant decision-making technologies. We will explore this in the following chapters.
